Debriefing, but Mini, but With an Update.

Whew, as of writing this update, it's been about a full semester since I put in this submission! If you found your way down to here, you must be really combing through what's around. Power to you.

This is the only section of this microsite that was updated in 2022. The rest of it is presented just as it was when I submitted it in 2022-SP, including the Ramble On that follows. This was a time when I felt up to my neck with class work. For me personally, I truly was. I stand firm for preserving my life balance between more productive matters like academics and work with leisure and fulfillment, and I wasn't meeting that balance for the bulk of that semester.

I was diagnosed with ADHD, primarily inattentive, about half a year later. Just a few weeks ago, actually, as of writing this. It wasn't a surprise. I started suspecting it as early as elementary school, but my parents dropped the subject. They weren't keen on getting me on a stimulant prescription at that age, so the matter was left unexplored all together. The bulk of my life, however, had been affected by my Social Anxiety Disorder and depressive symptoms. It made it challenging to confirm if I indeed had ADHD or not. Fortunately I was diagnosed with the previously mentioned disorders in 2018. Between therapy and medication, I achieved a headspace that allowed me to consider the possibility of ADHD more clearly.

So again, was it a surprise? Not really. But I believe it did give me enough to bring up with the DPRC. I'll be checking in with them sooner rather than later to see what resources may benefit me. And as with prior experience, it's allowed me to access medication and resources across the board that make things easier for me. I'd almost wager it'd make things "normal," whatever that is.

What's the takeaway from this experience and what's here? Probably along of these:

  • You're likely not as alone as you think. Seeking diagnoses on both occasions put me in situations that showed me people and communities succeeding regardless of these challenges. It's a big world and there's likely a group of people that have been where you are now. These sorts can often offer understanding and help in their own ways.

  • It's worth it to look for help if you want it. Mind you I didn't say if you "need" it. I know I personally have a tendency to put off seeking help until the last moment because "other people need it more than I do," among other reasons. But to the contrary, seeking help early on can prevent issues from developing to a breaking point.

  • Shit happens and that's okay. Between being dealt a bad hand or just encountering bad luck, things can get in the way when you least expect it. I might have too much faith in people, but I'd wager that's a fact of life that most are cognizant of at least from a personal perspective. I'm acutely aware that it's far easier said that done, but if you can manage it, please try to not beat yourself up when shit happens. Because it happens to everyone.

Debriefing, but Mini.

Wait, how did you get here?

Better yet, how did I get here?

Pretty early on in the DES roadmap, for both VCD and IND I'm pretty sure, is DES300. My professor had us take on a project that involved sewing. A lot of it. It was my first proper experience with a sewing machine.

To put it as gracefully as possible, it was excruciating and kicked me in an academic tardy hole I'm just barely beginning to dig myself out of.

Do I mention that for pity? Good grief, no, but it's pretty good tool to make a point.

Sneaking this project in the backdoor as part of my effort to dig myself out of said hole, despite only realizing in the midst of doing so that could've put an irreversible damper on my grade, gave me the chance to prepare for a critique question that seems to be consistently coming up: "If you had a bigger skillset, what would you have included in this site?" Now honestly because my pride demands I don't give the whole spiel verbally, chances are high I'll give an abridged version when the time comes to pass. This page is a neat opportunity to expand on that further.

At least in my circumstance, I don't really think "a bigger skillset" is the bottleneck here. I know I have a long ways to go before, I dunno, Silicon Valley Rockstar or something, but to my credit, I consider myself well adapted digitally. This sorta stuff comes fairly easy to me, and when it doesn't, I'm pretty well suited to being resourceful and figuring out how to leverage resources when it comes to getting the knowledge.

But what I don't have is time. I can already say I'm taking a reduced schedule because I know I prioritize building a time balance between my academics and literally everything else, even if that means spreading it out over a longer span of time. Yet even with that cushioning, one awry project was enough of a wrench in my system to break my roll for nearly a month. Not only does that sort of thing hurt me in the academics, I feel it hurts me in the reputation. Here's these people coming in an out with a standard issue load in two years and they get out relatively without a scratch? A yikes from me, dog.

It's the sort of issue that it feels like no amount of BuJo or pomodoro timers can resolve, and believe me I've tried them before. No, it doesn't directly answer the question of "If you had a bigger skillset what would you have included," but I hope it sheds some light on where I really feel I'm struggling. It ain't skillset, it ain't a single class being too much or too little. It's the whole lot taking too long. There's only 24 hours in a day and even less of them are waking hours.

Between you and me, I'm inclined to at the least stick with my gut and continue my four class, 12 unit load. But honestly, despite what seems to be ample protest from professors when the idea comes up, I'm inclined to push that to three classes, 9 units. That worked for me substantially well in DVC, and if I can carry it over to SF State with minimal consequences aside from the time span, I could dig that just fine.
